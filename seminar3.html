Theories of democracy: What should we expect from democratic innovations?

We need a definition of democracy

for two reasons:

  1. To clarify what we want democratic innovations to do.
  2. And to evaluate how well they meet those wishes.

Democracy = demos (the people) + kratos (rule) = "rule by the people"

But this is not very helpful without a clearer definiton of who the people are and how they are suppoosed to rule.

Democratic theory

  • A huge subject => We will only scratch the surface here.
  • Democratic theories are mostly normative.
  • The (main) point in this course is not to discuss the normative remits of different democratic theories.
  • But to use democratic theories as analytical frameworks.
  • Theories as different lenses to see the world through.

Main take-aways from this week

  • We need to clearly define democracy to be able to evaluate democratic innovations.
  • Democracy is a contested concept that is defined in many different ways.
  • We can use two different approaches to evaluate democratic innovations with theory:
    1. We can combine different theories to "combine the best from all".
    2. Democratic theory is needed to prioritise what is most important to democracy.

Some examples of democratic theories

  • Minimal/Schumpeterian Democracy
    • Main mechanism: Competetive elections for leaders.
    • Reasoning: Ordinary people are not good decision makers, but elections provide an incentive for rulers to not become tyrannical.
    • Main propnents: Schumpeter (1942)
  • Deliberative Democracy
    • Main mechanism: The force of the better argument should determine political outcomes (rather than the majority)
    • Reasoning: Under the right circumstances and conditions, all people are able to participate in reasoned debate, and agree on what is best for the common good.
    • Some proponents: Jürgen Habermas, James Fishkin, John Dryzek, and many more.
  • Participatory Democracy
    • Main mechanism: Direct citizen participation in decision-making at all levels.
    • Reasoning: Participation in politics is a good in itself, because it educates and empowers people.
    • Main proponents: Carole Pateman, (Jean-Jacques Rousseau, Sherry Arnstein)

Graham Smith's "democratic goods"

Taking an ecumenical approach: An analytical framework that can encompass all participatory institutions, ie. "democratic innovations" and democratic theories.

  • Inclusiveness
    • ...the degree to which each person has an equal right to participate and have their voice heard.
  • Popular control
    • ...the degree to which people are able to influence the different stages of decision making.
  • Considered judgement
    • ...the degree to which people are able to both understand the issues and see them from other people's perspective.
  • transparency
    • ...the degree to which the proceedings are open to public view.
  • Efficiency
  • Transferability

Discussion:

  • How easy or challenging do you think it is to apply these democratic goods to real cases of democratic innovations?
  • Which are the challenges of using this as an analytical framework?
  • Do you think it is a useful analytical framework?

Ian O'Flynn's critiques of Smith's democratic goods:

(As well as a general critique of ecumenical approaches)

  • Democratic goods so broad that they need further interpretation to be useful.
  • Different goods can be at odds with each other, so we need normative framework to prioritise between them.
  • The very definition of democratic innovations is democratically normative, as well as each of Smith's "goods" (even if they are presented as general goods).

=> We need a theory of democracy to clearly specify our normative expectations of democratic innovations.
