Mini-publics

What are they?

Clodagh Harris' definition:

"...a democratic innovation, in which a sub-group of citizens (some of whome may be randomly or quasi-randomly selected) engage with experts, witnesses, advocates, stakeholders and one another, in facilitated informed deliberations on a given matter, and publicly present their opinions and/or recommendations to their commissioning body and wider society." (p. 47)

Many different types of Mini-publics

But the all share a general process involving:

  1. An information phase.
  2. A deliberation phase.
  3. And some result based on this, in the form of a decision, recommendations, or reporting of insights.

Which democratic theory would you say mini-publics fits best with?

Discussion and reflection

Two questions

  1. If you were invited to participate in a mini-public would you say yes? Why?
  2. Would you trust a mini-publiic to represent you/make decisions on your behalf?
    • (or would you trust a mini-public more or less than elected representatives?)

That probably depends on a lot of factors.

Considering different cases, what would it take for you to want to participate in a MP or trust the outcomes of a MP you did not participate in?

  • Who - or what - should be represented in the MP? (ie. how should participants be recruited)
  • Which expertise should be presented to the participants? (witness testimonies, academic experts, interest groups)
  • How many times and how often should MP meet?
  • How big should the MP be?
  • Should participants be compensated for their time?
  • Is the process of deliberation important? (group discussions vs. plenum, majority decision vs. consensus, super majority, or meta consensus)
  • What should the facilitator(s)' most important role be?
